Guide

How this conversion works

Energy conversions here run through the joule as the SI base unit. Food labels in most countries use kilocalories (often just labeled 'Calories' with a capital C, which is a common source of confusion — a food 'Calorie' is actually a kilocalorie, i.e. 1,000 small calories).

Electricity bills are usually metered in kilowatt-hours (kWh), a unit of energy despite the 'watt' in its name — it's the energy used by a 1,000-watt appliance running for one hour. BTU (British Thermal Unit) commonly appears on air conditioner and heater specifications, especially in the US.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Is a food 'Calorie' the same as a science-class calorie?

No — a food Calorie (capital C, as printed on nutrition labels) is actually a kilocalorie, equal to 1,000 small calories (lowercase c) as used in chemistry. This converter's 'Calories' and 'Kilocalories' units are the same size for that reason.

How many kWh does a typical appliance use?

It depends entirely on the appliance's wattage and how long it runs — a 100-watt device running for 10 hours uses 1 kWh. Check the appliance's rated wattage and multiply by hours used, then divide by 1,000.
