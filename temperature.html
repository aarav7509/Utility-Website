Guide

How this conversion works

Temperature scales don't share a common zero point, so you can't convert between them by multiplying by a single factor the way you can with length or weight. Celsius is anchored to water's freezing point (0°) and boiling point (100°) at sea level; Fahrenheit uses 32° and 212° for the same two points; Kelvin starts at absolute zero, −273.15°C.

This converter applies the standard offset formulas shown in the table above, so a conversion like −40°C, which happens to equal exactly −40°F, comes out correctly rather than being approximated.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Why is −40 the same in Celsius and Fahrenheit?

It's the one point where the two linear scales cross. Solving (°F − 32) × 5/9 = °F for °F gives exactly −40, so it's a genuine mathematical coincidence of where each scale put its zero and step size.

Is Kelvin ever negative?

No. Kelvin measures from absolute zero, the coldest physically possible temperature, so 0 K is its floor. That's equal to about −273.15°C or −459.67°F.
